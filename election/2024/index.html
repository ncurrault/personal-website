Proposition 32: Raise minimum wage to $18/hour

YES
  • San Francisco has its own higher minimum wage anyway, so this is mostly about the rest of California.
  • My initial reaction here was confusion: I thought the current minimum wage law was indexed to inflation. Turns out it is, but it's capped at 3.5% annual increases. And this proposal will reinstate the CPI-based adjustments after the manual ramp up schedule to $18/hour.
  • I generally prefer stuff like this to be passed via legislature, but this has a provision where the legislature is still allowed to amend it (which is a problem with many initiative statues in general).
  • The math maths. Compounded inflation since 2020 works out to a 20.2% increase in prices, and the proposed increase to $17 in 2025 would bring it in line with this increase (21.4% net increase since 2020), so the real minimum wage is effectively identical. The next increase to $18 is impossible to gague because we don't know next year's inflation figures yet, however.

Proposition 33: Repeal Costa-Hawkins, allowing more local rent control

NO
  • Let's start by arguing against this without assuming any housing opinions. This same proposal has already been considered in 2018 and 2020. It failed resoundingly both times. If you don't get the answer you want at the ballot box, you can't just keep asking until you get the policy you want. It's a waste of public funds (printing these voter pamphlets isn't cheap) and voters' time. Passing something on the third try like this would set a terrible precedent.
  • This is shitty housing policy. California YIMBY has a good summary of why, but the TL;DR is that rent control is fine as long as recent construction is exempt. When rent control is imposed as soon as a new building is constructed, builders are much less certain that they can turn a profit, so it's harder to get an investor, and the building never happens. If you build in a buffer, investors are assured a finite period of (e.g.) 15 years. And after that point, rent control can solve displacement problems.
  • Our problem is that we don't have enough housing, and we need to build more. And this measure does nothing to encourage actually building apartments.
  • There is room for reforming Costa-Hawkins. It uses a static date in the 90s as a cutoff instead of a rolling one, which would really makes more sense. But there's progress in the legislature: they enacted statewide rent control that uses a rolling window, but its limits are kinda half-assed: they only cap it at 10% (or 5% plus inflation if that's lower).

Proposition 34: Fuck AHF

YES
  • This is the spiciest propositon on the ballot. It's essentially a bill micro-targeted at a controversial group known as the AIDS Healthcare Foundation (AHF). TL;DR if you hate the AHF vote YES, if you love them vote NO, and if you've never heard of them keep reading.
  • So what makes AHF so controversial?
    • AHF formed during in the late 80s at the peak of the HIV/AIDS crisis. They originally focused on hospice care, but as HIV treatments were discovered, they pivoted to offering various services to help prevent and treat HIV/AIDS. You may also be familiar with Out of the Closet, a thrift store they set up to help raise money. As of today, their reach has expanded globally, and they operate many clinics and pharmacies.
    • Although ostensibly a healthcare organization, AHF is also one of the largest landlords in Los Angeles. They operate these properties has led to them being accused of being a "slumlord". The LA times did a deep dive into issues with AHF-owned housing here.
    • The AHF and its president also advocate against building more housing. They sponsored Proposition 33 this election and its two similar predecessors that failed in previous elections. They also sued to block construction near AHF's headquarters that would block his view of the Hollywood Hills.
    • Even within the AIDS advocacy space, they have received pushback for their opposition to PrEP, a drug that helps prevent one from contracting HIV by inhibiting the functionality of reverse transcriptase enzyme that the virus uses to replicate. (Ok, I won't go further down this science rabbit hole because it's really not relevant, but the way PrEP works is pretty cool.)
    • The AHF receives a large amount of money from the federal 340B Drug Pricing Program. In a nutshell, the program "allows nonprofit health care providers serving low-income patients to buy prescription drugs from manufacturers at a discounted price. Providers can then charge insurers full price for the drugs — and pocket the difference." California is trying to close this loophole by having Medi-Cal Rx only cover the actual drug costs plus a small fee.
    • So to recap: they take public money from the subsidized drugs under 340B program and use it to buy apartment buildings and run controversial political campaigns.
  • OK, so what does this proposition actually do? It bans companies who rely on 340B funding from using 98% for anything except direct patient care. Huh, I wonder who that could apply to? (As far as anyone can tell, literally nobody but the AHF.)
  • Finally, regardless of how you feel about the AHF, there are still some (extremely valid) arguments for voting NO.
    • Editorial in the Chron
    • This sets a bad precedent. While the AHF is bad, it's not great to set a precedent of having interest-group squabbles play out on every ballot in the most populous state.
    • It's a blatant act of revenge for political speech. As the Voltaire quote goes, "I disapprove of what you say, but I will defend to the death your right to say it." The AHF has a right to advocate for shitty policy, and maybe we should defend that right to the death.
    • That said, I'm still a YES because (a) I'm really uncertain how another group could be targeted like this; AHF's funding model is one-of-a-kind and (b) while AHF has free speech, we do not need to spend Medi-Cal on that speech.

Proposition 35: Make existing tax permanant, using it to fund Medi-Cal services

YES
  • This is a budget set-aside to fund many Medi-Cal services. It has very broad support.
  • That said, there is some opposition. I feel that it does more harm than good by effectively keeping Medi-Cal funding from being in flux at annual budget negotiations. Additionally, due to some complicated ACA provisions, the tax needs to be approved by the federal government every time we change it, but this proposition has an escape valve (legislative supermajority) to amend it if needed to preserve federal medicaid funding.

Measure C: Inspector General

YES
  • Proponents argue more oversight is necessary to avoid corruption
  • Opponents argue that it just adds bureaucracy
  • It only "adds bureaucracy" in the form of a parallel method for detecting goverment problems, I really don't see much downside risk.
  • There is a need to increase government ethics oversight due to the recent DPW scandal, and I haven't heard any counter-proposals.
  • In GrowSF's redundancy argument, the only position that could really be viewed as redundant is the City Services Auditor, which this role can share Controller's Office staff with anyway.

Measure D: City Commissions and Mayoral Authority

YES
  • Removes bureaucracy
  • All counter-arguments in the pamphlet are people arguing that some minor commissions do important work, which they do. They can continue to do this work as advisory bodies or under corresponding departments.
  • We have twice as many commissions as comparable cities like Los Angeles, San Diego and San Jose.

Measure E: Creating a Task Force to Recommend Changing, Eliminating, or Combining City Commissions

LEAVE IT BLANK
  • This would set up a commission to recommend removing commissions. And it's a competing measure, so if both D and E pass, only the one with more votes takes effect.
  • Savvy voting on competing requires some interesting game theory due to an issue with our electoral system.
  • I am leaving it blank because I do feel that creating this task force would be better than nothing. The "Commission Impossible" Civil Grand Jury report did specifically recommend as much: "The rich irony of recommending a new commission to reduce the number of commissions is not lost on us. However we believe such a body is vital in order to optimize and streamline the city’s byzantine commission system."
  • If you are truly ambivalent between this and the status quo (or actively oppose it), consider a NO vote instead.
