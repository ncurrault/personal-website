Preamble

Voting is hard, especially in a place like San Francisco, California, where your ballot is an endless barrage of confusing ballot measures. So I figured I’d summarize all the research I did in the hopes that the hours I spent digging into City Controller reports and whatnot were not in vain.

Mayor

  1. London Breed (incumbent)
  2. Ahsha Safaí
  3. Mark Farrell
  4. Daniel Lurie

  • I get good vibes from Ahsha Safaí. Even though we disagree on some policy, I feel that he would be a good leader. Sort of a compromise candidate between the moderate and progressive factions of the city, and someone who knows how to build alliances from his work on the Board of Supervisors.   • Daniel Lurie is not qualified to be Mayor. He has zero government experience, and his major qualification appears to be building a single apartment building. Additionally, he has been running a barrage of attack ads on Farrell, which is a point against him because I don’t want to incentivize a negative campaign. Also I got bad vibes from him when watching the debate. That said, I’m still ranking him because he seems better than the last major candidate on housing...
  • Aaron Peskin is a NIMBY. He disputed this allegation in one of the debates, but I’m sorry, introducing legislation to reduce density in your district is practically the definition of NIMBYism. He also spent some debate time noting that he’s the only candidate in favor of Proposition 33.

Proposition 32: Raise minimum wage to $18/hour

YES
  • San Francisco has its own higher minimum wage anyway, so this is mostly about the rest of California.
  • My initial reaction here was confusion: I thought the current minimum wage law was indexed to inflation. Turns out it is, but it’s capped at 3.5% annual increases. And this proposal will reinstate the inflation-based adjustments after the manual ramp up schedule to $18/hour.
  • I generally prefer stuff like this to be passed via legislature, but this has a provision where the legislature is still allowed to amend it (which is a problem with many initiative statues in general).
  • The math maths. Compounded inflation since 2020 works out to a 20.2% increase in prices, and the proposed increase to $17 in 2025 would bring it in line wit this increase (21.4% net increase since 2020), so the real minimum wage is effectively identical. The next increase to $18 is impossible to gague because we don’t know next year’s inflation figures yet, however.

Proposition 33: Repeal Costa-Hawkins, allowing more local rent control

NO
  • Let’s start by arguing against this without assuming any housing opinions. This same proposal has already been considered in 2018 and 2020. It failed resoundingly both times. If you don’t get the answer you want at the ballot box, you can’t just keep asking until you get the policy you want. It’s a waste of public funds (printing these voter pamphlets isn’t cheap) and voters’ time. Passing something on the third try like this would set a terrible precedent.
  • This proposition would simply repeal the Costa-Hawkins Rental Housing Act, a state law that (among other things) prohibits local jurisdictions from imposing rent control on properties built after 1995. It also prohibits vacancy control, a stronger form of rent control in which the rent increase between successive tenants is also limited.
  • This is shitty housing policy. California YIMBY has a good summary of why, but the TL;DR is that rent control is fine as long as recent construction is exempt. When rent control is imposed as soon as a new building is constructed, builders are much less certain that they can turn a profit, so it’s harder to get an investor, and the building never happens. If you build in a buffer, investors are assured a finite period of (e.g.) 15 years. And after that point, rent control can solve displacement problems.
  • NIMBYs in Huntington Beach are already strategiziing on how to use this to block housing if it passes.
  • Our problem is that we don’t have enough housing, and we need to build more. And this measure does nothing to encourage actually building apartments.
  • There is room for reforming Costa-Hawkins. It uses a static date in the 90s as a cutoff instead of a rolling one, which would really makes more sense. But there’s progress in the legislature: they enacted statewide rent control that uses a rolling window, but its limits are kinda half-assed: they only cap it at 10% (or 5% plus inflation if that’s lower).

Proposition 34: Fuck the AHF in particular

YES
  • This is the spiciest propositon on the ballot. It’s essentially a bill micro-targeted at a controversial group known as the AIDS Healthcare Foundation (AHF). TL;DR if you hate the AHF vote YES, if you love them vote NO, and if you’ve never heard of them keep reading.
  • So what makes AHF so controversial?
    • AHF formed during in the late 80s at the peak of the HIV/AIDS crisis. They originally focused on hospice care, but as HIV treatments were discovered, they pivoted to offering various services to help prevent and treat HIV/AIDS. You may also be familiar with Out of the Closet, a thrift store they set up to help raise money. As of today, their reach has expanded globally, and they operate many clinics and pharmacies.
    • Although ostensibly a healthcare organization, AHF is also one of the largest landlords in Los Angeles. They operate these properties has led to them being accused of being a “slumlord”. The LA times did a deep dive into issues with AHF-owned housing here.
    • The AHF and its president also advocate against building more housing. They sponsored Proposition 33 this election and its two similar predecessors that failed in previous elections. They also sued to block construction near AHF’s headquarters that would block his view of the Hollywood Hills.
    • Even within the AIDS advocacy space, they have received pushback for their opposition to PrEP, a drug that helps prevent one from contracting HIV by inhibiting the functionality of reverse transcriptase enzyme that the virus uses to replicate. (Ok, I won’t go further down this science rabbit hole because it’s really not relevant, but the way PrEP works is pretty cool.)
    • The AHF receives a large amount of money from the federal 340B Drug Pricing Program. In a nutshell, the program “allows nonprofit health care providers serving low-income patients to buy prescription drugs from manufacturers at a discounted price. Providers can then charge insurers full price for the drugs — and pocket the difference.” California is trying to close this loophole by having Medi-Cal Rx only cover the actual drug costs plus a small fee.
    • So to recap: they take public money from the subsidized drugs under 340B program and use it to buy apartment buildings and run controversial political campaigns.
  • OK, so what does this proposition actually do? It bans companies who rely on 340B funding from using 98% for anything except direct patient care. Huh, I wonder who that could apply to? (As far as anyone can tell, literally nobody but the AHF.)
  • Finally, regardless of how you feel about the AHF, there are still some (extremely valid) arguments for voting NO.
    • Editorial in the Chron
    • This sets a bad precedent. While the AHF is bad, it’s not great to set a precedent of having interest-group squabbles play out on every ballot in the most populous state.
    • It’s a blatant act of revenge for political speech. As the Voltaire quote goes, “I disapprove of what you say, but I will defend to the death your right to say it.” The AHF has a right to advocate for shitty policy, and maybe we should defend that right to the death.
    • That said, I’m still a YES because (a) I’m really uncertain how another group could be targeted like this; AHF’s funding model is one-of-a-kind and (b) while AHF has free speech, we do not need to spend Medi-Cal on that speech.

Proposition 35: Make existing tax permanant, using it to fund Medi-Cal services

YES
  • This is a budget set-aside to fund many Medi-Cal services. It has very broad support.
  • That said, there is some opposition. I feel that it does more good things than bad things by effectively keeping Medi-Cal funding from being in flux at annual budget negotiations. Additionally, due to some complicated ACA provisions, the tax needs to be approved by the federal government every time we change it, but this proposition has an escape valve (legislative supermajority) to amend it if needed to preserve federal medicaid funding.

Proposition 36: Reform Prop 47

NO
  • The history of how this got on the ballot is worth pausing to consider: it was initially a joint effort from the California District Attorneys Association and the California Retailers Association. But the Retailers effectively withdrew once they were able to negotiate with the legislature to enact a package of laws targeted at organized retail theft and fencing operations (partially with the threat of this proposition as a bargaining chip).
    • Notably, this package closes the “theft spree loophole” by allowing the aggregation of the values of stolen goods stolen from multiple locations to meet felony thresholds.
  • The measure would essentially introduce a “three strikes” system for crimes like drug possession and shoplifting, both of which are currently misdemeanors. It also introduces a treatment-focused court with mandatory that could prove useful in combating the fentanyl crisis
  • Increased prison populations will have a huge financial cost. Prop 47 used savings from prison operations for funding programs for helping crime victims; this proposal could endanger those funds.
  • Proponents make some dubious claims
    • “In California, criminal justice reforms have advanced equity and reduced incarceration rates. But the unintended consequences of these policies include an epidemic of drug use, trafficking, and repeat retail theft because the people committing these crimes don’t face any serious consequences.”
    • Criminology studies using synthetic control group method is unable to find evidence of an increase of crimes as a result of Prop 47.
    • Prop 47 also likely reduced recidivism.
  • Crime rates are decreasing and have been for years; I just don’t see the need for a change like this one. Sure, SF feels unsafe at times and stuff is locked up, but I would argue that this is mostly because of organized retail theft (addressed by the aforementioned legislative package) and lack of enforcement of existing laws.
  • This proposition has some interesting ideas (like the treatment-mandated felony concept), but it just doesn’t seem like the right time. Especially since the legislative retail package was introduced, so it would be wiser to wait for that to play out before determining if a reversion to three-strikes laws is warranted.

Local Measures

Measure A: Schools Improvement and Safety Bond

YES
  • Standard infrastructure bond with broad support

Measure B: Community Health and Medical Facilities, Street Safety, Public Spaces, and Shelter to Reduce Homelessness Bond

YES
  • Standard infrastructure bond with broad support
  • Again, GrowSF and the League agree so it seems pretty uncontroversial

Measure C: Inspector General

YES
  • Proponents argue more oversight is necessary to avoid corruption
  • Opponents argue that it just adds bureaucracy
  • It only “adds bureaucracy” in the form of a parallel method for detecting goverment problems, I really don’t see much downside risk.
  • There is a need to increase government ethics oversight due to the recent Department of Public Works scandal, and I haven’t heard any counter-proposals.
  • In GrowSF’s redundancy argument, the only position that could really be viewed as redundant is the City Services Auditor, which this role can share Controller’s Office staff with anyway.

Measure D: City Commissions and Mayoral Authority

YES
  • Removes bureaucracy
  • All counter-arguments in the pamphlet are people arguing that some minor commissions do important work, which they do. They can continue to do this work as advisory bodies or under corresponding departments.
  • We have twice as many commissions as comparable cities like Los Angeles, San Diego and San Jose.

Measure E: Creating a Task Force to Recommend Changing, Eliminating, or Combining City Commissions

LEAVE IT BLANK
  • This would set up a commission to recommend removing commissions. And it’s a competing measure, so if both D and E pass, only the one with more votes takes effect.
  • Savvy voting on competing requires some interesting game theory due to a loophole with our electoral system that the Board of Supervisors is exploiting here.
  • I am leaving it blank because I do feel that creating this task force would be better than nothing. The “Commission Impossible” Civil Grand Jury report did specifically recommend as much: “The rich irony of recommending a new commission to reduce the number of commissions is not lost on us. However we believe such a body is vital in order to optimize and streamline the city’s byzantine commission system.”
  • If you are truly ambivalent between this and the status quo (or actively oppose it), consider a NO vote instead.

Measure F: Police Staffing and Deferred Retirement

NO
  • This measure seeks to solve a problem: SFPD is understaffed. The proposed mechanism is a deferred returement option (DROP) program that would allow senior officers on the brink of retirement to effectively delay their retirement a couple of years in exchange for double pay during this period (receiving both their salary and pension payments, albeit in a deferred account). It would also impose some restrictions on police officers who opt into the DROP program, including a requirement that they are actively involved in patrols or investigations and that they are not promoted while in their semi-retired state.
  • SF has tried this before: a nearly-identical DROP program was implemented in 2008, and it didn’t work. The Board of Supervisors abandoned it following a pessimistic report from the city Controller indicating that it was more expensive than anticipated. Admittedly, this proposal attempts to resolve the prior iteration’s budget issues by limiting eligibility, but the DROP program was used very infrequently last time, so these changes seem like they would only further reduce usage.
  • Finally, this just fails the smell test for me. Why should we pay someone double their salary for a couple of years at the end of their career? This simply cannot be a permanent solution to the relevant issue worthy of a Charter Amendment.

Measure G: Funding Rental Subsidies for Affordable Housing Developments Serving Low Income Seniors, Families, and Persons with Disabilities

NO
  • This is just a budget dispute between the Board of Supervisors and the Mayor that should be hashed out with the standard process
  • The mayor didn’t want to budget as much as the Board of Supervisors wanted, so they went around her and are seeking to amend the city charter to establish a minimum funding level for this program.
  • The program itself is worth funding, but this only serves to remove flexibility and obstruct the city’s legislative process.

Measure H: Retirement Benefits for Firefighters

NO
  • Lowers firefighter retirement age from 58 to 55
  • There’s a reason we raised it to 58 a few years ago: the retirement system was insolvent during the Great Recession. If we lower the age again, we’re going to have to handle the same crisis a second time.
  • The argument that the retirement ages are inequitable is ridiculous. The safest way to raise a retirement age is to have the change take effect only for new hires such that nobody is denied benefits that they were expecting for years.
  • This measure’s proponents bill the increased age as a temporary artifact from a Great-Recession era policy, but it wasn’t. Retirement age was raised as part of a broader pension reform in November 2011 Prop C. The proponent argument back then argued that “Prop C is the consensus plan that ensures all city employees share the burden in bad economic times and enjoy the benefits in good times.” Which still seems to ring true to me: bad economic times can impact the pension, and I don’t want to end up right back where we were at the next recession.
  • Everybody loves firefighters, but this proposal doesn’t make financial sense.

Measure I: Retirement Benefits for Nurses and 911 Operators

YES
  • Allows part-time nurses and 911 dispatchers to buy into a city retirement system in an effort to make it easier for them to become full-time workers
  • Seems like a decent idea worth trying, prop has broad support (GrowSF and the League agree)
  • Opponents argue that it doesn’t fix the true barrier to hiring full-time nurses (SF bureaucracy in hiring)

Measure J: Funding Programs Serving Children, Youth, and Families

YES
  • Seems fine, unanimously referred to ballot, no real voter guide opposition or even a rebuttal/argument against submitted.

Measure K: Close Upper Great Highway to Cars

YES
  • This is a fun area to ride a bike in during the weekend closures; it honestly works better as a park.

Measure L: Tax rideshares and Waymo to fund Muni

YES
  • Do the math for yourself: what is 4% of your monthly rideshare spending? For me, it was a trivial amount that I am definitely willing to give Muni
  • Muni is facing a budget crisis, and I feel that this is an elegant solution: any rideshare trip that I take is essentially a failure of the public transit system, so this redirects a bit of funds from these trips to make transit a bit better.

Measure M: Changes to Business Taxes

LEAVE IT BLANK
  • This is another fun game theory problem! This measure competes with L, but not vice versa. In other words, if both L and M pass and L gets move votes, both go into effect. But if M gets more votes, only M goes into effect.
  • This prop itself is a good idea. It essentially modifies the business tax code to be more friendly to small business, and the Controller’s Summary indicates that it will not have a significant impact on revenue in the long term.

Measure N: First Responder Student Loan and Training Reimbursement Fund

YES
  • Sets up a fund for student loan forgiveness but doesn’t actually fund it. It’s possible that it could receive private donations though.
  • Probably just a weird political statement that doesn’t do anything, but it really can’t hurt. If it ends up funded, this could be a valuable recruiting tool for the City.

Measure O: Supporting reproductive rights

YES
  • Reproductive rights are not particularly under threat here, but this is more of a statement against efforts in red states.
  • Minimal cost to essentialy confirm that crisis pregnancy centers cannot exist in the City.
